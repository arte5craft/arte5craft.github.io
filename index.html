Overview

Helping stroke survivors
walk again independently and inexpensively

GaitWay is a wearable assistive device developed as part of one of the projects at SUTD. It targets one of the most common challenges in post-stroke rehabilitation: the inability to adequately lift the leg during the swing phase of walking, often caused by hip flexor weakness.

The core innovation is a capstan-drive mechanism — a system where a rope wound around a drum amplifies the torque of a compact motor. This allows a single small actuator to generate forces large enough to meaningfully assist human gait, without the bulk or cost of industrial exoskeleton platforms.

Design challenge: Achieve a minimum of 40 Nm output torque for reliable leg lifting, packaged in a wearable form factor, using a single motor system, within a $350 component budget.

Engineering Approach

Key design
decisions

Every major choice in GaitWay involved a trade-off between performance, cost, weight, and wearability. Below are the critical decisions that shaped the final system.

01

Capstan Drive over Gear Train

A traditional gear train was considered but rejected due to weight, backlash, and cost. A capstan (rope-on-drum) drive achieves high mechanical advantage through multiple wraps, is nearly frictionless at low loads, and can be manufactured from inexpensive printed components. The rope tension scales linearly with wrap angle — enabling predictable torque amplification.

02

Dyneema over Steel Cable or Nylon

Dyneema (ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene) was selected for the transmission rope after evaluating creep behaviour under sustained load. Steel cable added unacceptable weight; nylon exhibited creep that caused positional drift during extended wear. Dyneema's near-zero creep, high tensile strength-to-weight ratio, and flexibility made it the optimal choice for repeatable actuation cycles.

03

Body-Mounted, Not Frame-Mounted

Rather than attaching to an external rigid frame (which limits mobility and adds bulk), GaitWay mounts directly to the user's body using a harness. This keeps the form factor compact, allows natural gait patterns, and reduces overall system weight. The actuator rides at the hip, transmitting force to the thigh via a guided Dyneema loop.

04

Single Motor Architecture

Constraining to a single motor kept costs under the $350 limit while forcing elegant mechanical design. The capstan geometry was calculated to deliver the required torque from a 24V brushless DC motor rated at 12 Nm stall torque, with a 4:1 rope mechanical advantage yielding the 48 Nm target at the leg attachment point.

Results

What we
achieved

The completed GaitWay system met all primary design targets. During final demonstrations, the device successfully assisted a human subject through a full walking cycle at natural pace, with the wearer reporting a clearly perceptible reduction in effort during the swing phase.

The project was recognised with the Breakthrough Innovation Award at SUTD's EPD Term 4 showcase — awarded for its combination of thoughtful constraint-driven engineering, novel application of capstan mechanics to wearable rehabilitation, and real demonstrated functionality.
